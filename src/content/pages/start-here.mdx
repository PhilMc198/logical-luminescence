Nobody knows what the future holds.

This has always been true, and there's no particular reason to think it will stop being true now. The range of opinion on artificial intelligence and autonomous systems runs from "it's all vastly overhyped" to "you cannot possibly overstate what's coming" — and serious, informed people sit at every point along that spectrum.

I want to be honest about where I stand. I tend toward expecting massive societal change from these technologies. If I didn't, I wouldn't be doing this. But I hold that view with some humility, because nobody — including me — knows for certain how this unfolds.

What I do think is that intellectual honesty requires us to take the question seriously. If you're confident it's all hype, that's a legitimate position — but it means believing your assessment is more likely correct than the many credible voices who disagree. That's fine if you have cogent reasons for thinking those voices are wrong. Less fine if the confidence is simply comfortable. When experts in a field make claims, those claims carry more weight than the rest of ours. When many experts converge on similar warnings, the prudent response is to open our ears and start thinking through the implications — if only as a thoughtful exercise.

Prudence, I think, is the right frame here. It is better to prepare for possibilities that may not eventuate than to ignore possibilities that do. The cost of preparation — examined, considered, grounded preparation — is relatively low. The cost of being caught unprepared for a civilisational shift is not.

That's the disposition this site comes from. Not certainty. Not alarm. Just the sense that the question deserves serious attention — inwardly, technologically, and politically.

## What I think is happening

Beyond the uncertainty about scale and speed, there is a structural argument that I think deserves to be named plainly. Artificial intelligence and autonomous robotics are not simply productivity tools. They represent a shift in the economic foundations of society comparable to — and probably exceeding — the industrial revolution. That kind of shift doesn't just change what people do for work. It destabilises institutions, concentrates wealth and power, and creates conditions in which existing political structures come under enormous strain.

History suggests that when those structures fail to manage that strain, the response is rarely more freedom. It tends, instead, toward control.

That is not a partisan observation. It is a structural one. And I think it deserves to be named plainly, because understanding it changes what "adapting to AI" actually means.

## What you'll find here

This site is organised around four connected ideas.

The first is that the inner life matters more, not less, in this moment — that contemplative practice, self-knowledge, and values clarity are not retreats from the world but navigation tools within it.

The second is that honest, non-hyped assessment of the technology itself is necessary — what it actually does, what it can't, and what its proliferation means in practice.

The third is that the political and structural stakes need to be part of this conversation, not bracketed off as too difficult or too divisive.

The fourth is that adaptation is possible — that individuals and communities can find genuine agency and flourishing within the transition, if they approach it with their eyes open.

These aren't separate topics. They are a single argument, built from the inside out.

## Who this is for

This site isn't for people who have already decided. It's for people who are still working it out — who find both the AI-utopian and the AI-doomer framings unsatisfying, who suspect the truth is more complicated and more personal than either, and who want a space to think seriously without being lectured at.

You don't need a particular politics to be here. You do need a willingness to hold difficult questions without demanding quick answers. And probably some tolerance for the possibility that your relationship to technology, to power, and to your own inner life are all connected — and that examining one will inevitably implicate the others.

If that sounds like where you are, you're in the right place.
